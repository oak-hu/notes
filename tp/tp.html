0  The big picture

Topology drops the metric and keeps the open sets. Continuity becomes "preimages of opens are open," and the three great global properties — connectedness, compactness, and (for quotients/surfaces) Hausdorffness — are preserved by the right maps. Two spines carry the course (red): compactness → "compact→Hausdorff cts bijection is a homeomorphism" → identifying quotient spaces, and simplicial complexes → closed combinatorial surfaces → the classification theorem.

1  Topological spaces, continuity, separation

DefTopological space; from a metric; examples (1.1–1.11)
  • Topology $\mathcal T$ on $X$: $\varnothing,X\in\mathcal T$; closed under finite intersections and arbitrary unions (1.1). Members = open sets. (Infinite intersections of opens need not be open.)
  • Metric ⇒ topology (1.6): the $\varepsilon$-ball-open sets form a topology $\mathcal T_d$. $X$ is metrizable if $\mathcal T=\mathcal T_d$ for some metric.
  • The counterexample reservoir (1.7): discrete (all subsets open, metrizable), indiscrete $\{\varnothing,X\}$, cofinite ($U$ open iff $X\setminus U$ finite). Indiscrete is coarsest, discrete finest.
DefContinuity, homeomorphism (1.20–1.26)
$f:X\to Y$ is cts if $f^{-1}(V)$ is open for every open $V$ (equivalently $f^{-1}(\text{closed})$ closed, 1.21). Homeomorphism = cts bijection with cts inverse. Compositions of cts maps are cts (1.22).
  • Sequences are weaker here: cts ⇒ sequentially cts (1.24), but the converse can fail in general spaces (1.25) — sequences don't see the topology.
  • Lemma 1.26 (workhorse): $U$ open $\iff$ every $x\in U$ has an open $U_x$ with $x\in U_x\subseteq U$.
Def+PropSeparation axioms: T1 and Hausdorff (1.46–1.53)
  • T1 (1.46): distinct points each have an open set excluding the other $\iff$ every singleton is closed (1.47).
  • Hausdorff / T2 (1.48): distinct points have disjoint open nbhds. Every metric space is Hausdorff (1.49).
  • Prop 1.52: in a Hausdorff space, limits of sequences are unique.
  • Separation axioms are pulled back by injective cts maps (1.50), hence are homeomorphism invariants (1.51).
  • T1 is not enough for unique limits (1.53): an infinite cofinite space is T1 but a distinct-term sequence converges to every point. Hausdorff is the right hypothesis.

1′  Closure/interior; subspace; basis; product

Def+PropClosure, interior, boundary, dense, limit points (1.27–1.45)
  • $\overline A$ = smallest closed superset (1.28); pointwise (1.30): $x\in\overline A\iff$ every open $U\ni x$ meets $A$. $\mathring A$ = largest open subset (1.38); $\partial A=\overline A\setminus\mathring A=\overline A\cap\overline{X\setminus A}$ (1.44).
  • $\overline{X\setminus A}=X\setminus\mathring A$ (1.41) — closure and interior are dual via complement.
  • Metric only (1.35): $\overline A$ = limits of sequences in $A$; limit points $A'$ = limits of distinct-term sequences. (Fails for general spaces.)
  • $f$ cts $\iff f(\overline A)\subseteq\overline{f(A)}$ for all $A$ (1.36).
Def+PropSubspace topology (1.54–1.59)
$\mathcal T_A=\{A\cap U:U\in\mathcal T\}$; the inclusion is cts (1.55). Closed-in-$A$ = $A\cap(\text{closed in }X)$ (1.56). Closure in $A$ = $\overline B^X\cap A$; interior in $A$ contains $\mathring B^X$, possibly strictly (1.57–1.58).
  • Caution: $(0,1)\times\{0\}$ has empty interior in $\mathbb R^2$ but is open in the line $\mathbb R\times\{0\}$. Openness is relative to the ambient space.
Def+PropBasis; product topology (1.60–1.74)
  • Basis $\mathcal B$ (1.60): opens are unions of basis sets. A family with (B1) covering $X$ and (B2) $B\cap B'$ a union of basis sets generates a topology (1.64). Test continuity on a basis (1.63).
  • Product topology (1.66): basis $\{U\times V\}$. Not every open set is a rectangle. On $\mathbb R^n$ it is the standard topology (1.70). Preserves T1/Hausdorff (1.71).
  • Universal property (1.72–1.73): projections are cts and the product is the coarsest such topology; $(f,g):Z\to X\times Y$ is cts $\iff$ both components are. (The model for the quotient universal property later.)

1″  Connectedness & path-connectedness

Def+PropConnectedness (1.76–1.85)
Connected (1.76): not a disjoint union of two non-empty opens. TFAE (1.77): connected $\iff$ only clopen sets are $\varnothing,X$ $\iff$ every cts map to $\{0,1\}$ is constant — the workhorse criterion.
The connectedness toolkit (all via the $\{0,1\}$ criterion)
  • Cts image of connected is connected (1.80): pull back a disconnection.
  • Union with a common point / common connected core is connected (1.81, 1.82): a cts map to $\{0,1\}$ is constant on each piece, and they agree on the overlap.
  • Product: $X\times Y$ connected $\iff$ both are (1.83) — glue the slice $X\times\{y_0\}$ to the fibres $\{x\}\times Y$ via 1.82.
  • Closure: $A$ connected, $A\subseteq B\subseteq\overline A$ ⇒ $B$ connected (1.85).
Def+PropPath-connectedness (1.86–1.91)
Path (1.86): cts $\gamma:[0,1]\to X$. Path-connected (1.88): any two points joined by a path.
Prop 1.89: path-connected ⇒ connected.
Sketch
  • $[0,1]$ is connected (interval), so each path image is connected (1.80); they all share the basepoint $a$, so their union $=X$ is connected (1.82 with core $\{a\}$).
  • Converse false (1.90): the topologist's-sine-curve-type space is connected but not path-connected.
  • Cts image of path-connected is path-connected (1.91).

2  Compactness

DefCompact; FIP; first facts (2.1–2.12)
  • Compact (2.3): every open cover has a finite subcover. A subset is compact in the subspace topology (= every cover by opens-of-$X$ has a finite subcover).
  • FIP form (2.4): compact $\iff$ every family of closed sets with the finite-intersection property has non-empty total intersection (De Morgan dual).
  • Heine–Borel (2.9): $[a,b]$ is compact. Non-examples (2.10): $(a,b)$, $[a,\infty)$.
  • Closed in compact ⇒ compact (2.11, via FIP). Converse needs separation: a singleton is always compact but need not be closed (2.12).
Prop+CorCompactness meets Hausdorff (2.13–2.18)
Hyp: $X$ Hausdorff, $K\subseteq X$ compact.
A point outside $K$ is separated from $K$ by disjoint opens (2.13) — so compact ⊆ Hausdorff ⇒ closed (2.14). In a metric space compact ⇒ closed and bounded (2.17–2.18).
Sketch of 2.13/2.14
  • For each $y\in K$ take disjoint opens $U_y\ni y$, $V_y\ni x$; cover $K$, extract finite $U_{y_1},\dots,U_{y_m}$; then $\bigcap V_{y_i}$ is an open nbhd of $x$ missing $K$. So $X\setminus K$ is open.
Prop+CorContinuous images; EVT (2.19–2.22)
Cts image of compact is compact (2.19): pull back a cover, take a finite subcover, push forward. Hence Extreme Value Theorem (2.21): a cts $f:X\to\mathbb R$ on a compact $X$ is bounded and attains its bounds (image is compact $\subseteq\mathbb R$, so closed and bounded).
ThmProducts; general Heine–Borel (2.23, 2.24)
2.23: $X\times Y$ compact $\iff$ both $X,Y$ compact. 2.24 (Heine–Borel in $\mathbb R^n$): a subset of $\mathbb R^n$ is compact $\iff$ closed and bounded.
Sketch
  • Tube lemma: for each $y$, finitely many boxes cover the slice $X\times\{y\}$; intersect their $V$-parts to a tube $X\times V_y$. Cover $Y$ by the $V_y$ (compactness of $Y$), take finitely many — finitely many boxes in all.
  • 2.24: a closed bounded set sits in a box $[-r,r]^n$, which is a finite product of compact $[-r,r]$ (Heine–Borel + 2.23); closed inside compact ⇒ compact (2.11).
  • Warning: "closed + bounded ⇒ compact" is special to $\mathbb R^n$ — false in a general metric space (e.g. an infinite-dimensional normed space).
ThmCompact → Hausdorff: cts bijection is a homeomorphism (2.25, 2.26)
Hyp: $X$ compact, $Y$ Hausdorff, $f:X\to Y$ cts bijection.
Then $f$ is a homeomorphism.
Sketch — the single most useful tool in the course
  • Show $f$ is a closed map: $V$ closed in $X$ ⇒ $V$ compact (2.11) ⇒ $f(V)$ compact (2.19) ⇒ $f(V)$ closed in $Y$ (2.14, Hausdorff). So $f^{-1}$ is cts.
  • Each hypothesis pays its way: compactness of $X$ feeds 2.11+2.19, Hausdorffness of $Y$ feeds 2.14. Drop either and it fails.
  • This is the engine for identifying quotient spaces (§3) and for "$|K|$ is determined up to homeomorphism" (§4).

2′  Sequential compactness; Lebesgue numbers, ε-nets

Def+ThmSequential compactness; Bolzano–Weierstrass; metric equivalence (2.28–2.36)
Seq. compact (2.28): every sequence has a convergent subsequence. 2.29: Bolzano–Weierstrass — in a compact space every infinite set has an accumulation point; a compact metric space is seq. compact. 2.30: compact metric ⇒ complete. 2.31: a seq. compact metric space is compact — so for metric spaces, compact $\iff$ sequentially compact.
Sketch of seq. compact ⇒ compact (2.31), the substantial direction
  • Lebesgue number (2.32): for any open cover of a seq. compact metric space there is $\varepsilon\gt0$ such that every $\varepsilon$-ball lies in a single cover set. (Else build $x_n$ with $B(x_n,1/n)$ in no set; a convergent subsequence lands in some cover set's interior — contradiction.)
  • Finite $\varepsilon$-net (2.36): total boundedness — else greedily pick an $\varepsilon$-separated sequence, which has no Cauchy (hence no convergent) subsequence.
  • Combine: cover by the finitely many $\varepsilon$-balls of a net, each inside a cover set ⇒ finite subcover.

3  Quotient spaces

Def+PropQuotient topology; quotient maps; universal property (3.1–3.35)
  • Quotient topology on $X/\mathcal R$: $U$ open $\iff p^{-1}(U)$ open, where $p:X\to X/\mathcal R$ collapses. It is the finest topology making $p$ cts (3.28).
  • Universal property (3.9): $g:X/\mathcal R\to Z$ is cts $\iff g\circ p$ is cts — this is how you build maps out of a quotient.
  • Saturated sets & separation (3.17–3.21): open/closed sets of $X/\mathcal R$ ↔ open/closed saturated sets of $X$; $X/\mathcal R$ is T1 $\iff$ classes are closed; Hausdorff $\iff$ distinct classes lie in disjoint open saturated sets.
  • Quotient map (3.32): surjective with "$U$ open $\iff p^{-1}(U)$ open." Prop 3.35: a quotient map $q:X\to Y$ realises $Y\cong X/\mathcal R$ for $\mathcal R$ = its fibre partition — the practical recognition tool.
PropIdentifying a quotient (the compactness payoff) (3.11)
Hyp: $f:X\to Y$ cts surjection, $X$ compact, $Y$ Hausdorff; $\mathcal R$ = fibre partition $\{f^{-1}(y)\}$.
Then $X/\mathcal R\cong Y$.
Sketch
  • $f$ factors as $X\xrightarrow{p}X/\mathcal R\xrightarrow{\bar f}Y$ with $\bar f$ a cts bijection (universal property 3.9); $X/\mathcal R$ is compact (cts image of $X$), $Y$ Hausdorff, so $\bar f$ is a homeomorphism by 2.25.
  • Standard identifications: $[0,1]/\{0\sim1\}\cong S^1$; $[0,1]^2$ with edge gluings $\cong$ torus / Klein bottle; $\mathbb D^2/\partial\cong S^2$; $\mathbb R/\mathbb Z\cong S^1$, $\mathbb R^n/\mathbb Z^n\cong T^n$ (3.36).
ExReal projective space $\mathbb{RP}^n$ (3.24–3.27)
  • $\mathbb{RP}^n=(\mathbb R^{n+1}\setminus\{0\})/(x\sim\lambda x)$ = lines through $0$; Hausdorff (3.25, via disjoint open saturated cones) and compact (image of $S^n$).
  • Models of $\mathbb{RP}^2$ (3.26): $S^2/(\text{antipodal})$; the disc $\mathbb D^2$ with antipodal boundary points glued; the square with both edge-pairs flipped. (All identified via 3.11.)
  • Quotients can be nasty: $\mathbb R/\mathbb Q$ is indiscrete (3.16) — a Hausdorff space can have a non-Hausdorff quotient.

4  Simplicial complexes

DefSimplices, abstract complexes, realisation, triangulation (4.2–4.11)
  • Standard $n$-simplex $\Delta^n=\{x\in\mathbb R^{n+1}:x_i\ge0,\sum x_i=1\}$; faces ↔ non-empty subsets of vertices.
  • Abstract simplicial complex $(V,\Sigma)$ (4.5): $\Sigma$ = non-empty finite vertex-subsets, containing all singletons and closed under taking non-empty subsets.
  • Realisation $|K|$ (4.6): glue a copy of $\Delta^{|\sigma|-1}$ for each simplex $\sigma$ along face inclusions (a quotient of a disjoint union of simplices). A triangulation of $X$ is a homeomorphism $|K|\to X$ (4.8); e.g. the boundary tetrahedron triangulates $S^2$.
  • Caution (4.10): in a complex a vertex set spans at most one simplex — the naive 2-square "torus" is not a simplicial complex (needs ≥7 vertices).
Lem+PropProperties of realisations (4.15–4.24)
  • Subcomplex realisations are closed (4.15); the star $\mathrm{st}(v)$ is open (4.18, its complement is the subcomplex spanned by the other vertices).
  • Edge-connected $\iff$ connected (4.20): $|K|$ connected $\iff$ any two vertices are joined by an edge path.
  • Finite $\Rightarrow$ compact (4.21, cts image of finitely many simplices), injects into $\mathbb R^N$ (4.22), hence (with 2.25) metrizable (4.23) and Hausdorff (4.24).
Why 4.23/4.24 — the compactness payoff again
  • A cts injection $|K|\hookrightarrow\mathbb R^N$ from a compact space into a Hausdorff space is a homeomorphism onto its image (2.25/2.26); the image inherits a metric ⇒ $|K|$ metrizable ⇒ Hausdorff.

5  Surfaces & the classification theorem

Def+PropManifolds, surfaces, polygons with side identifications (5.1–5.6)
  • $n$-manifold (5.1): Hausdorff, locally homeomorphic to open sets of $\mathbb R^n$. A surface is a 2-manifold (5.3).
  • Polygon with a complete set of side identifications: even-sided polygon, sides glued in pairs (encoded by a boundary word). Prop 5.6: the result is always a closed (compact) surface — check disc nbhds at inside/edge/vertex points.
ThmClassification of closed combinatorial surfaces (5.15–5.27)
Closed combinatorial surface (5.15): connected finite simplicial complex in which every vertex link is a simplicial circle. Then every edge is in exactly two triangles and $|K|$ is a surface (5.16).
Thm 5.18: every closed combinatorial surface is homeomorphic to exactly one of $$M_g\ (g\ge0,\ \text{sphere}+g\ \text{handles}),\qquad N_h\ (h\ge1,\ \text{sphere}+h\ \text{crosscaps}).$$
Sketch — boundary-word reduction
  • Step 0 (5.19): cut $K$ into a single $2n$-gon with edges paired (a boundary word).
  • Free moves (5.20): reverse / cyclically permute the word. 5.26: cancel an adjacent inverse pair $xx^{-1}$ (shortens the word → induction). 5.27: reduce to a single vertex equivalence class.
  • Collect crosscaps (5.21): $xAxB\mapsto xxA^{-1}B$ pulls each "same-orientation" pair together into a crosscap $xx$.
  • Collect handles (5.23): interlocking reversed pairs $x\dots y\dots x^{-1}\dots y^{-1}$ become commutator blocks $xyx^{-1}y^{-1}$.
  • Key trade (5.25): a handle adjacent to a crosscap becomes three crosscaps ($xxyzy^{-1}z^{-1}\mapsto xxyyzz$). So if any crosscap is present, all handles convert — landing in $N_h$; otherwise pure handles give $M_g$. (Klein bottle $aba^{-1}b\cong aabb=N_2$, Ex 5.22.)
Def+ThmEuler characteristic distinguishes the surfaces (5.28–5.31)
$\chi(K)=\sum(-1)^i n_i = V-E+F$ (5.29) is a topological invariant (5.30, $\chi$ independent of triangulation — proved in Algebraic Topology). For the standard surfaces (5.31): $$\chi(M_g)=2-2g,\qquad \chi(N_h)=2-h.$$

Hypothesis cheat-sheet

The recurring exam trap: which results need Hausdorff, which need compact, which need a metric?

HypothesisWhat it's needed for
Hausdorffunique limits (1.52); compact ⇒ closed (2.14); compact→Hausdorff cts bijection is a homeo (2.25); $|K|$ Hausdorff (4.24); surfaces are Hausdorff by definition. Cofinite (infinite) is T1 but not Hausdorff — the standard counterexample.
compactcts image compact / EVT (2.19–2.21); closed-in-compact (2.11); the homeo criterion (2.25); identifying quotients (3.11); $|K|$ finite ⇒ compact (4.21).
metric (not just topological)compact $\iff$ sequentially compact (2.29/2.31); closure = sequential closure (1.35); Lebesgue number & ε-nets (2.32/2.36). In general spaces sequences don't determine the topology.
connected vs path-connectedpath-conn ⇒ conn always (1.89); converse only with extra hypotheses (open in a normed space) — the sine-curve is the gap.
closed + bounded ⇒ compactonly in $\mathbb R^n$ (Heine–Borel 2.24) — fails in infinite-dimensional metric spaces.
vertex links are circlesmakes a finite complex a closed combinatorial surface (5.15/5.16) — feeds the classification theorem.
