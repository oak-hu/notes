1  Classifying & counting finite abelian groups

Recipe
  1. Factor the order $n=p_1^{e_1}\cdots p_k^{e_k}$.
  2. Number of groups $=\prod_i p(e_i)$, where $p(e)$ is the number of partitions of $e$ (each partition of $e_i$ = a way of splitting the $p_i$-part into cyclic pieces).
  3. Primary form: for each prime, a partition $e_i=a_1+a_2+\cdots$ gives factors $\mathbb Z/p_i^{a_1}\oplus\mathbb Z/p_i^{a_2}\oplus\cdots$ — as many summands as possible.
  4. Invariant-factor form (the canonical form): line up each prime's powers in decreasing order in columns; multiply across each column. The products $d_1\mid d_2\mid\cdots\mid d_r$ ($d_r$ rightmost/largest) are the invariant factors — as few summands as possible. Convert between the two with the CRT.
$p(1)=1,\ p(2)=2,\ p(3)=3,\ p(4)=5,\ p(5)=7$ (memorise the small ones).
Worked — counting How many abelian groups of order $675$? $\;675=3^3\cdot5^2$, so the count is $p(3)\cdot p(2)=3\cdot 2=\ans{6}$. The six (primary form), from partitions $3=3,\,2{+}1,\,1{+}1{+}1$ and $2=2,\,1{+}1$: $$\mathbb Z/27\oplus\{\mathbb Z/25\text{ or }\mathbb Z/5\oplus\mathbb Z/5\},\quad (\mathbb Z/9\oplus\mathbb Z/3)\oplus\{\dots\},\quad (\mathbb Z/3)^3\oplus\{\dots\}.$$ Order $216=2^3\cdot3^3$: $\;p(3)\cdot p(3)=3\cdot3=\ans{9}$.
Worked — invariant factors Put $\mathbb Z/60\oplus\mathbb Z/21$ in invariant-factor form.
  1. Primary pieces: $60=2^2\cdot3\cdot5\Rightarrow \mathbb Z/4,\mathbb Z/3,\mathbb Z/5$; $\;21=3\cdot7\Rightarrow\mathbb Z/3,\mathbb Z/7$. So the prime-power pile is $\{2^2;\ 3,3;\ 5;\ 7\}$.
  2. Largest power of each prime $\to$ top invariant factor: $d_2=2^2\cdot3\cdot5\cdot7=420$.
  3. Leftover ($3$) $\to d_1=3$. Check $d_1\mid d_2$: $3\mid420$ ✓.
$$\mathbb Z/60\oplus\mathbb Z/21\;\cong\;\ans{\mathbb Z/3\oplus\mathbb Z/420}\qquad(\text{check }3\cdot420=1260=60\cdot21).$$
Past papers 2014 Q3b ($\mathbb Z/60\oplus\mathbb Z/21$), 2016 Q3d (order 675), 2018 Q3a (order 216), 2021 Q3a (from a presentation).

2  Arithmetic in $\mathbb Z[i]$ and $\mathbb Z[\sqrt d]$

Recipe
  • Norm. On $\mathbb Z[\sqrt d]$, $N(a+b\sqrt d)=|a^2-db^2|$; on $\mathbb Z[\sqrt{-d}]$ ($d\gt0$), $N(a+b\sqrt{-d})=a^2+db^2$. It is multiplicative: $N(zw)=N(z)N(w)$.
  • Units $\iff N=1$ (for imaginary $d$). So $u$ is a unit $\iff N(u)=1$.
  • Irreducibility test: if $N(z)$ is a prime integer, $z$ is irreducible (a proper factor would have norm a proper divisor of a prime).
  • Euclidean (for $\mathbb Z[i]$): to divide $s$ by $t$, compute $s/t\in\mathbb C$ and round each coordinate to the nearest integer $\to q$; then $N(s-qt)\le\tfrac12 N(t)\lt N(t)$.
Worked — units Units of $\mathbb Z[\sqrt{-3}]$. Need $a^2+3b^2=1$ with $a,b\in\mathbb Z$: forces $b=0,a=\pm1$. So $\mathbb Z[\sqrt{-3}]^\times=\ans{\{\pm1\}}$. (For $\mathbb Z[i]$: $a^2+b^2=1\Rightarrow\{\pm1,\pm i\}$. For real $d$, e.g. $\mathbb Z[\sqrt2]$, $a^2-2b^2=\pm1$ has infinitely many solutions — Pell — so infinitely many units.)
Worked — sum of two squares (the 2025 set-piece) Write $p=13$ as a sum of two squares, via $\mathbb Z[i]$. (General $p\equiv1\bmod4$.)
  1. $\mathbb Z_p^\times$ is cyclic of order $p-1\equiv0\bmod4$, so it has an element of order 4, i.e. $\exists\,m$ with $m^2\equiv-1\bmod p$. Here $m=5$: $\;5^2=25\equiv-1\bmod13$.
  2. Then $p\mid m^2+1=(m+i)(m-i)$ in $\mathbb Z[i]$, but $p\nmid m\pm i$ (the imaginary part $1/p\notin\mathbb Z$). So $p$ is not prime in $\mathbb Z[i]$.
  3. $\mathbb Z[i]$ is a Euclidean domain, hence a UFD, so $p=\pi\bar\pi$ with $N(\pi)=p$. Find $\pi=\gcd(p,\,m+i)$ by the Euclidean algorithm: $\gcd(13,\,5+i)=3-2i$.
  4. $N(3-2i)=3^2+2^2=13$, so $\;13=(3-2i)(3+2i)=\ans{3^2+2^2}.$
(Smaller check: $p=5$, $m=2$ ($2^2=4\equiv-1$), $5=1^2+2^2$.)
Worked — non-unique factorisation Show factorisation is not unique in $\mathbb Z[\sqrt{-5}]$. $$6=2\cdot3=(1+\sqrt{-5})(1-\sqrt{-5}).$$ Norms: $N(2)=4,\ N(3)=9,\ N(1\pm\sqrt{-5})=6$. Each factor is irreducible: a proper factor of $2$ would have norm $2$, of $3$ norm $3$, of $1\pm\sqrt{-5}$ norm $2$ or $3$ — but $a^2+5b^2\in\{2,3\}$ is impossible. And $2$ is not an associate of $1\pm\sqrt{-5}$ (units are only $\pm1$). So these are two genuinely different factorisations into irreducibles. (Equivalently: $2,3$ are irreducible but not prime.) The same trick: $4=2\cdot2=(1+\sqrt{-3})(1-\sqrt{-3})$ in $\mathbb Z[\sqrt{-3}]$.
Past papers $\mathbb Z[i]$ Euclidean: 2016, 2017, 2023, 2025. Sum of two squares: 2025 Q2c. Units / non-unique factorisation in $\mathbb Z[\sqrt{-d}]$: 2022 Q1, 2024 Q1.

3  Polynomial irreducibility & factorisation over $\mathbb Q$

Recipe By Gauss, factoring in $\mathbb Q[x]$ = factoring in $\mathbb Z[x]$ (clear denominators to content 1). Tools, in order of cheapness:
  • Rational root test: any root $p/q$ (lowest terms) has $p\mid a_0$, $q\mid a_n$. For degree 2 or 3, no rational root $\Rightarrow$ irreducible.
  • Reduction mod a prime $\ell$ (with $\ell\nmid a_n$): if $\bar f$ is irreducible in $\mathbb F_\ell[x]$ then $f$ is irreducible over $\mathbb Q$. (One-way.)
  • Eisenstein at $\ell$: $\ell\mid a_0,\dots,a_{n-1}$, $\ell\nmid a_n$, $\ell^2\nmid a_0$ $\Rightarrow$ irreducible. Often after a substitution $x\mapsto x+1$.
  • Substitution: $y=x^2$ for biquadratics; $x\mapsto x+1$ for cyclotomics.
Worked — substitution Factorise $6x^4+5x^2+1$ over $\mathbb Q$. Put $y=x^2$: $\,6y^2+5y+1=(2y+1)(3y+1)$, so $$6x^4+5x^2+1=\ans{(2x^2+1)(3x^2+1)}.$$ Each factor has no rational (indeed no real) root, so is irreducible over $\mathbb Q$.
Worked — rational root & mod $p$
  • $x^3+5x^2+3x+2$: candidate roots $\pm1,\pm2$ give $11,3,36,8$ — none zero. Degree 3, no root $\Rightarrow$ irreducible.
  • $x^3-x-1$: reduce mod $2$: $x^3+x+1\in\mathbb F_2[x]$ has $\bar f(0)=\bar f(1)=1\ne0$, so no root, so irreducible in $\mathbb F_2[x]$ $\Rightarrow$ irreducible over $\mathbb Q$.
Worked — Eisenstein
  • $x^3-2$: Eisenstein at $\ell=2$ ($2\mid2$, $2\nmid1$, $4\nmid2$) $\Rightarrow$ irreducible, so $2^{1/3}\notin\mathbb Q$.
  • Cyclotomic $\Phi_p=1+x+\cdots+x^{p-1}=\dfrac{x^p-1}{x-1}$: substitute $x=y+1$. Then $\Phi_p(y+1)=\dfrac{(y+1)^p-1}{y}=y^{p-1}+\binom p1 y^{p-2}+\cdots+\binom{p}{p-1}$, every middle coefficient $\binom pk\,(0\lt k\lt p)$ is divisible by $p$, constant term $\binom p1=p$, and $p^2\nmid p$. Eisenstein at $p$ $\Rightarrow\Phi_p$ irreducible.
Past papers 2024 Q2b ($6x^4+5x^2+1$, two cubics, cyclotomic), 2021 Q1a (Eisenstein, state & apply), 2014 Q2 (content/Gauss + rational root + irreducibility tests).

4  Smith normal form & the structure of $\mathbb Z^n/\mathrm{im}(A)$

Recipe
  1. Use elementary row and column operations over $\mathbb Z$ (swap; add an integer multiple of one row/col to another) to reduce $A$ to a diagonal $\mathrm{diag}(d_1,\dots,d_r)$ with $d_1\mid d_2\mid\cdots\mid d_r$ — the Smith normal form. (Pivot on a smallest-$|{\cdot}|$ entry; if it doesn't divide a neighbour, do a division-with-remainder step to shrink it; repeat, then clear its row & column and recurse on the smaller block.)
  2. Read off the structure: if the columns of $A$ are the relations among generators of $\mathbb Z^n$, then $\mathbb Z^n/\mathrm{im}(A)\cong\bigoplus_i\mathbb Z/d_i$ (drop $d_i=1$; each $d_i=0$ contributes a free $\mathbb Z$).
  3. Sanity check: for a square $A$, $\;|\det A|=\prod_i d_i$ (when no $d_i=0$).
Worked Find the Smith normal form of $A=\begin{pmatrix}1&2&3\\4&5&6\\7&8&10\end{pmatrix}$ and hence the group $\mathbb Z^3/\mathrm{im}(A)$.
  1. Pivot $a_{11}=1$. Clear column 1: $R_2-4R_1,\ R_3-7R_1$ $\to\begin{pmatrix}1&2&3\\0&-3&-6\\0&-6&-11\end{pmatrix}$.
  2. Clear row 1: $C_2-2C_1,\ C_3-3C_1$ $\to\begin{pmatrix}1&0&0\\0&-3&-6\\0&-6&-11\end{pmatrix}$.
  3. Work on the $2\times2$ block $\begin{pmatrix}-3&-6\\-6&-11\end{pmatrix}$. Do $C_3-2C_2$: $\to\begin{pmatrix}-3&0\\-6&1\end{pmatrix}$.
  4. The entry $1$ is a new pivot: swap it to the corner and clear, leaving $\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&-3\end{pmatrix}\sim\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&3\end{pmatrix}$.
So $\mathrm{SNF}(A)=\ans{\mathrm{diag}(1,1,3)}$, and $\;\mathbb Z^3/\mathrm{im}(A)\cong\ans{\mathbb Z/3}$. Check: $\det A=-3=\pm(1\cdot1\cdot3)$ ✓.

A chain example: $\begin{pmatrix}2&-2&4\\2&4&2\\6&6&12\end{pmatrix}\xrightarrow{\text{SNF}}\mathrm{diag}(2,2,12)$, so the cokernel is $\mathbb Z/2\oplus\mathbb Z/2\oplus\mathbb Z/12$ (invariant factors $2\mid2\mid12$); check $\det=48=2\cdot2\cdot12$ ✓.
Past papers 2016 Q3c (basis of $\mathbb Z^3$ adapted to a submodule), 2022 Q3a (diagonalise over a ED + uniqueness), 2023 Q3b (SNF, also over $\mathbb Z[i]$), 2021 Q3a (group from a presentation).

5  Rational & Jordan canonical forms

Recipe View $V=k^n$ with operator $A$ as a $k[t]$-module ($t$ acts as $A$). Then:
  1. Compute the Smith normal form of $tI-A$ over $k[t]$; its non-unit diagonal entries are the invariant factors $f_1\mid f_2\mid\cdots\mid f_m$ (monic).
  2. $V\cong\bigoplus_i k[t]/(f_i)$; the characteristic polynomial is $\prod_i f_i$ and the minimal polynomial is $f_m$ (the largest).
  3. RCF $=$ block-diagonal of the companion matrices $C(f_i)$. [$C(t^n+c_{n-1}t^{n-1}+\cdots+c_0)$ has sub-diagonal $1$'s and last column $-(c_0,\dots,c_{n-1})^{\mathsf T}$.]
  4. Over an algebraically closed field, split each $f_i$ into $(t-\lambda)^a$ factors and use Jordan blocks $J_a(\lambda)$ instead — that's the Jordan form.
Worked — RCF from char/min poly $A=\mathrm{diag}(2,2,3)$. Characteristic polynomial $(t-2)^2(t-3)$; minimal polynomial $(t-2)(t-3)$ (distinct eigenvalues, $A$ diagonalisable). Invariant factors $f_1\mid f_2$ with $f_1f_2=$ char and $f_2=$ min: $$f_1=t-2,\qquad f_2=(t-2)(t-3)=t^2-5t+6.$$ $$\text{RCF}=\;C(f_1)\oplus C(f_2)=\;\bigl(2\bigr)\ \oplus\ \begin{pmatrix}0&-6\\1&5\end{pmatrix} =\ans{\begin{pmatrix}2&0&0\\0&0&-6\\0&1&5\end{pmatrix}}.$$ (Jordan form here is just $\mathrm{diag}(2,2,3)$, since $A$ is already diagonal.)
Worked — the 2023 "$A^2=-I$" question $A\in M_2(\mathbb R)$ with $A^2+I=0$. Then $A$ satisfies $t^2+1$, which is irreducible over $\mathbb R$, so the minimal polynomial is $t^2+1$ $=$ characteristic polynomial. One invariant factor $t^2+1$, so $$\text{RCF}=C(t^2+1)=\ans{\begin{pmatrix}0&-1\\1&0\end{pmatrix}}.$$ The change of basis: pick any $v\ne0$ and set $E=[\,v\mid Av\,]$. Then $A\cdot v=Av$ and $A\cdot(Av)=A^2v=-v$, so in the basis $\{v,Av\}$ the matrix of $A$ is exactly $\begin{pmatrix}0&-1\\1&0\end{pmatrix}=E^{-1}AE$. (Over $\mathbb C$ instead, $t^2+1=(t-i)(t+i)$, so the Jordan form is $\mathrm{diag}(i,-i)$.)
Worked — straight from $tI-A$ For $A=\begin{pmatrix}0&1&0\\0&0&1\\-1&-2&-3\end{pmatrix}$, reducing $tI-A$ over $\mathbb Q[t]$ gives $\mathrm{diag}\bigl(1,1,\;t^3+3t^2+2t+1\bigr)$: a single invariant factor, so $A$ is already the companion matrix of $t^3+3t^2+2t+1$ and equals its own RCF.
Past papers 2017 Q3 (cyclic vector, min=char, compute RCF of $aT+bI$, $T^{-1}$), 2018 Q3b ($M_A\cong M_B\iff$ similar), 2019 Q3b ($\mathbb Q^3$ as a $\mathbb Q[x]$-module), 2023 Q3a.

6  Field degrees $[\mathbb Q(\alpha):\mathbb Q]$ and the structure of $\mathbb Q(\alpha)$

Recipe
  • For $\alpha$ algebraic over $F$, find the minimal polynomial $m_\alpha$ (monic, least degree, irreducible — check with §3 tools). Then $F(\alpha)\cong F[x]/(m_\alpha)$, $\;[F(\alpha):F]=\deg m_\alpha$, with $F$-basis $\{1,\alpha,\dots,\alpha^{\deg m_\alpha-1}\}$.
  • Tower Law: $[L:F]=[L:K]\,[K:F]$ for $F\subseteq K\subseteq L$. Build big extensions one generator at a time.
Worked — simple extensions
  • $[\mathbb Q(\sqrt2):\mathbb Q]=2$ ($m=x^2-2$); $\;[\mathbb Q(2^{1/3}):\mathbb Q]=3$ ($m=x^3-2$, Eisenstein at 2).
  • Field of fractions of $\mathbb Z[\sqrt n]$: it is $\mathbb Q(\sqrt n)$, of degree $\ans{2}$ over $\mathbb Q$ — unless $n$ is a perfect square, when $\sqrt n\in\mathbb Q$ and the degree is $1$.
Worked — tower law $[\mathbb Q(\sqrt2,\sqrt3):\mathbb Q]=?$ Tower $\mathbb Q\subseteq\mathbb Q(\sqrt2)\subseteq\mathbb Q(\sqrt2,\sqrt3)$:
  1. $[\mathbb Q(\sqrt2):\mathbb Q]=2$.
  2. $[\mathbb Q(\sqrt2,\sqrt3):\mathbb Q(\sqrt2)]=2$: $x^2-3$ stays irreducible over $\mathbb Q(\sqrt2)$ because $\sqrt3\notin\mathbb Q(\sqrt2)$ (if $\sqrt3=a+b\sqrt2$ then squaring gives $3=a^2+2b^2+2ab\sqrt2$, forcing $ab=0$ and then $\sqrt3\in\mathbb Q$ or $\sqrt{3/2}\in\mathbb Q$ — both false).
  3. Tower law: $[\mathbb Q(\sqrt2,\sqrt3):\mathbb Q]=2\cdot2=\ans{4}$.
In fact $\mathbb Q(\sqrt2,\sqrt3)=\mathbb Q(\sqrt2+\sqrt3)$, and $\sqrt2+\sqrt3$ has minimal polynomial $x^4-10x^2+1$ (degree 4, consistent).
