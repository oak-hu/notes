Rouché's theorem: where the textbook proof has a gap, and the simplest repair

Re: CA notes, Theorem 3.6.3. Setup: $f,g$ holomorphic, $\gamma$ a simple contour with its interior in $U$, and $|f|>|g|$ on $\gamma^\ast$. Put $$h=\frac{f+g}{f}=1+\frac{g}{f}.$$ Write $\operatorname{ord}_a(\cdot)$ for the vanishing order at a point $a$ (positive $=$ zero, negative $=$ pole, $0$ $=$ neither), and let $N_f,\,N_{f+g}$ be the number of zeros inside $\gamma$, counted with multiplicity.

The sentence is true — but only one way

The notes say: "its zeros are zeros of $f+g$ and its poles are zeros of $f$." As implications these are correct:

$$a \text{ a pole of } h \;\Longrightarrow\; \operatorname{ord}_a(h)<0 \;\Longrightarrow\; \operatorname{ord}_a(f)>\operatorname{ord}_a(f+g)\ge 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; a \text{ a zero of } f,$$ $$a \text{ a zero of } h \;\Longrightarrow\; \operatorname{ord}_a(h)>0 \;\Longrightarrow\; \operatorname{ord}_a(f+g)>\operatorname{ord}_a(f)\ge 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; a \text{ a zero of } f+g.$$

The trouble is the converse. A zero of $f+g$ need not be a zero of $h$: if $f$ also vanishes there to equal-or-higher order, the quotient cancels and $\operatorname{ord}_a(h)\le 0$. So the map "zeros/poles of $h$" $\leftrightarrow$ "zeros of $f+g$ / zeros of $f$" is not a multiplicity-preserving bijection, and the proof's jump from

$$Z_h = P_h \qquad\text{(the winding-number argument)}$$

to "$\,f$ and $f+g$ have the same number of zeros$\,$" is not justified by the quoted sentence.

Sharpest witness: $g=f/2$. Then $|g|=\tfrac12|f|<|f|$ (hypothesis holds), but $h=\tfrac{3}{2}$ is constant: no zeros, no poles. Yet every zero of $f$ is an equal-order zero of $f+g=\tfrac32 f$. So here every zero of $f+g$ is one of the "unaccounted" coincident zeros — none of them is a zero of $h$.

The simplest repair: count net order, not zeros-vs-poles

Don't decompose $h$ into its zeros and poles at all. The one identity you need holds pointwise at every $a$, with no exceptions for coincidences, because $h=(f+g)/f$:

$$\operatorname{ord}_a(h) \;=\; \operatorname{ord}_a(f+g) \;-\; \operatorname{ord}_a(f).$$

The argument principle gives the signed count $Z_h-P_h=\sum_a \operatorname{ord}_a(h)$. Sum the identity over all $a$ inside $\gamma$ and split:

$$Z_h - P_h \;=\; \sum_a \operatorname{ord}_a(h) \;=\; \underbrace{\sum_a \operatorname{ord}_a(f+g)}_{=\,N_{f+g}} \;-\; \underbrace{\sum_a \operatorname{ord}_a(f)}_{=\,N_f} \;=\; N_{f+g}-N_f.$$

The winding-number argument from the notes still shows the left side is $0$ (on $\gamma^\ast$, $|g/f|<1$, so $\Gamma=h\circ\gamma$ lies in $B(1,1)\subset\{\operatorname{Re}>0\}$, and a principal branch of $\log$ closes up: $\oint_\Gamma dz/z = 0$). Hence

$$N_{f+g}-N_f \;=\; 0.$$

A coincident zero contributes $+\operatorname{ord}_a(f+g)$ to $N_{f+g}$ and $+\operatorname{ord}_a(f)$ to $N_f$; in the difference these cancel exactly as much as they do in $\operatorname{ord}_a(h)$. Nothing is lost, precisely because we only ever use the net order. In the $g=f/2$ case: $0-0$ on the left, $N_{f+g}-N_f=k-k=0$ at each zero on the right. Consistent.

Equivalent one-line phrasing (no $h$ decomposition at all)

If you prefer to never mention zeros/poles of $h$: the logarithmic derivative is additive, $\dfrac{(f+g)'}{f+g}-\dfrac{f'}{f}=\dfrac{h'}{h}$, so

$$\underbrace{\frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_\gamma \frac{(f+g)'}{f+g}}_{N_{f+g}} \;-\; \underbrace{\frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_\gamma \frac{f'}{f}}_{N_f} \;=\; \frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_\gamma \frac{h'}{h} \;=\;0.$$

Here $N_{f+g}$ and $N_f$ are each computed by the argument principle applied to the genuinely holomorphic $f+g$ and $f$ independently — shared zeros counted correctly in each — and $h$ appears only to certify the difference vanishes. The "zero of both" worry cannot even arise.

(Hypothesis $|f|>|g|$ on $\gamma^\ast$ is what makes all three integrals legal: $|f+g|\ge |f|-|g|>0$ and $|f|>0$ on $\gamma^\ast$, so neither $f$ nor $f+g$ vanishes on the contour, and $\Gamma$ avoids $0$.)
